/// 01

Eighth Wonder of the World

The Mausoleum of Emperor Qinshihuang is located at the northern foot of Mount Li in Lintong District, Xi'an, Shaanxi Province. It is the burial site of Ying Zheng, the first emperor in Chinese history.

In 1974, the discovery of the Terracotta Warriors astonished the world. The three pits cover a total area of over 20,000 square meters, containing approximately 8,000 terracotta warriors and horses, and tens of thousands of bronze weapons. Each warrior is unique — with different hairstyles, armor, and facial expressions — faithfully recreating the mighty formation of the Qin imperial army.

Craftsmanship of the Great Qin

The craftsmanship of the Terracotta Warriors is breathtaking. Each figure was hand-sculpted, from headdresses and armor down to shoes, with extraordinary detail.

Archaeological discoveries confirm that the warriors were originally painted in vibrant colors — vermillion, pinkish green, ochre, purple, and more — which faded after over two millennia underground. The three pits are laid out in a precise formation: Pit 1 is an infantry phalanx, Pit 2 is a mixed formation of chariots and crossbowmen, and Pit 3 is the command headquarters, together presenting the complete military system of the Qin dynasty.

Half a Century of Archaeological Legend

In the spring of 1974, villagers in Xiyang Village, Lintong, accidentally discovered fragments of terracotta figures while digging a well. The lead archaeologist Yuan Zhongyi and his team conducted in-depth excavations, unveiling the mystery of this underground legion that had slept for over two thousand years.

Over fifty years of archaeological work, three generations of researchers have carried the torch forward. In 2023, the results of the third excavation of Pit 1 were announced — over 200 new warriors were discovered, and the formation patterns were preliminarily clarified. Each new discovery of the Terracotta Warriors rewrites our understanding of Qin civilization.
