World's Largest Dinosaur Egg Fossil Cluster

Xixia Dinosaur Relic Park is the site of the world's largest discovery of dinosaur egg fossils, with tens of thousands of fossilized eggs unearthed. In terms of quantity, variety, and preservation quality, these dinosaur egg fossils are unparalleled anywhere on Earth.

Stepping into the Dinosaur Egg Fossil Museum feels like traveling back to the Jurassic Era hundreds of millions of years ago. Thousands of dinosaur egg fossils are arranged in neat rows, telling visitors the mysteries of ancient life. This is not only a sacred research site for paleontologists but also an unmissable wonderland for anyone fascinated by prehistoric civilization.

Funiu Mountain's Main Peak Laojieling

Laojieling is one of the main peaks of the Funiu Mountains, standing at 2,212 meters above sea level with majestic and ever-changing scenery. As the highest point of the Funiu range, it offers panoramic views of the rolling mountains stretching for hundreds of miles, with seas of clouds swirling like a fairyland.

Standing atop Laojieling, you can gaze upon the endless mountain ranges and feel the heroic spirit of "rising to the summit and seeing all mountains at a glance." The mountain is covered with dense primeval forests, towering ancient trees, and fresh air, making it a natural oxygen bar and summer retreat.

Climate Divide Line

Laojieling marks the climate divide between northern and southern China as well as the watershed between the Yangtze and Yellow Rivers. Standing on this natural boundary, you can experience the unique feeling of "one foot in the north, one foot in the south — witnessing all of China in a single day."

The climate and vegetation on either side of the mountain are strikingly different: the southern slope is warm and humid with lush plant life, while the northern slope is cool and dry with a more rugged landscape. This fascinating geographical phenomenon makes Laojieling a natural laboratory for studying the climatic differences between northern and southern China.
