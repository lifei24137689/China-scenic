Shang Capital
/// 01

Shang Dynasty Capital

Yin Xu is located in Anyang City, Henan Province — the capital site of the late Shang Dynasty, with a history of over 3,300 years. Since King Pangeng of the Shang moved the capital here in 1300 BC, it served as the political, economic, and cultural center through eight generations and twelve kings for 255 years, representing the most prosperous period of the Shang Dynasty.

The Yin Xu site is grand in scale, covering a total area of approximately 30 square kilometers. In 1899, the discovery of oracle bone script shocked the world, transforming Yin Xu from legend into documented history. Formal archaeological excavations began in 1928, making it the birthplace of modern Chinese archaeology. It was inscribed on the World Heritage List in 2006.

Oracle Bone Script
/// 02

Oracle Bone Script

Oracle bone script is the earliest known and most systematic mature writing system in China, named for being inscribed on turtle shells and animal bones. In 1899, Wang Yirong, a libationer of the Imperial Academy of the Qing Dynasty, accidentally discovered carved symbols on oracle bones, thus unveiling the study of oracle bone script.

Approximately 150,000 inscribed oracle bones have been unearthed at Yin Xu, with about 4,500 individual characters identified, of which roughly 1,500 have been deciphered. Oracle bone script records rich content of Shang Dynasty politics, economy, military affairs, rituals, astronomy, and calendrical science, earning the title "China's Earliest Book."

Bronze Civilization
/// 03

Bronze Civilization

Yin Xu has yielded tens of thousands of bronze artifacts, representing the pinnacle of China's Bronze Age. The national treasure Simuwu Ding (also known as Houmuwu Ding) weighs 832.84 kg, making it the heaviest bronze artifact ever discovered in China and the heaviest in the world.

The bronzes of Yin Xu are diverse in type, including ritual vessels, weapons, tools, and chariot fittings — dignified in form and exquisitely decorated. The taotie motifs and kuilong dragon patterns on ritual vessels in particular showcase the extraordinary artistic creativity of Shang craftsmen, serving as tangible testimony to the early brilliance of Chinese civilization.
