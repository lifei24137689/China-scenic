/// Pine and Dragon Vein

Dragon Vein Unfolds · The Emperor's Choice

The Western Qing Tombs are situated at the foot of Yongning Mountain in Yi County, Baoding City. They represent the last imperial mausoleum complex built by the Qing dynasty after entering the Shanhai Pass. Construction began in 1730 during the reign of Emperor Yongzheng with the Tailing Mausoleum and continued for over 180 years, culminating in the Chongling Mausoleum during the Guangxu period.

The tomb complex comprises 14 mausoleums, including 4 imperial tombs, 3 empress tombs, 3 imperial consort gardens, and tombs for princes and princesses. Emperors Yongzheng, Jiaqing, Daoguang, and Guangxu are buried here. With 402 architectural structures, the complex is grand in scale and complete in its system.

In 2000, the Western Qing Tombs were inscribed on the UNESCO World Heritage List as an outstanding representative of the Ming and Qing imperial mausoleums.

/// Tailing Main Tomb

Yongzheng's Rest · Majestic Grandeur

Tailing is the first and largest mausoleum of the Western Qing Tombs, boasting the most complete architectural system. Emperor Yongzheng broke with the tradition of "a son follows his father's burial site" and chose to establish a new tomb area at Yongning Mountain in Yi County, thus beginning the history of the Western Tombs.

In front of Tailing stands a magnificent stone archway with five openings, six pillars, and eleven eaves, featuring exquisite carvings. The 2.5-kilometer-long Sacred Way is lined with stone statues, ornamental pillars, and other ceremonial structures, creating an atmosphere of solemn grandeur. The Hall of Eminent Favor shines with splendor, showcasing imperial dignity.

The architectural standards of Tailing profoundly influenced the later Changling of Emperor Jiaqing, Muling of Emperor Daoguang, and Chongling of Emperor Guangxu, establishing the overall layout of the Western Qing Tombs.

/// Stone Carving Art

Royal Craftsmanship · Exquisite Perfection

The stone carving art of the Western Qing Tombs epitomizes the highest standards of Qing imperial architecture. The tomb area is adorned with exquisite stone archways, stone statues, ornamental pillars, stele pavilions, and stone offering tables, each a masterfully crafted artistic treasure.

The stone statues include lions, elephants, horses, warriors, and civil officials, characterized by dignified postures, smooth lines, and varied expressions. The ornamental pillars feature coiled dragons soaring through clouds, while the stele pavilions house bixi carrying stone tablets — all showcasing the superb craftsmanship and imperial grandeur of Qing stone carving.

These stone carvings are not merely architectural decorations but also carriers of history and culture, recording the evolution of the Qing imperial mausoleum system and the splendor of royal art.
