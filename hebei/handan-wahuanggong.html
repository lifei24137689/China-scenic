/// 001_ORIGIN

Ancestral Temple · Millennia-old Foundation

Wahuang Palace sits atop a Neolithic site dating back 10,000 years. It consists of five areas: the Entrance Zone, Butian Garden, Butian Lake, Wahuang Palace, and Butian Valley, covering an area of 5 square kilometers. As the largest Nüwa worship site in China, it is an important base for studying the root ancestor culture of the Chinese nation.

Wahuang Palace is located on the cliffs of Zhonghuang Mountain in She County, Handan City. It was first built during the Northern Qi Dynasty (550 AD) and has a history of nearly 1,500 years. The entire complex follows the mountain's natural contours, skillfully integrating with its surroundings, hailed as an outstanding example of ancient Chinese architectural art, carrying the Chinese nation's reverence for the human ancestor Nüwa.

Every year on the 18th day of the third lunar month, Nüwa's birthday, grand ceremonial activities are held here. Chinese people from home and abroad gather to pay homage to the Chinese ancestor, inheriting the bloodline roots of Chinese civilization.

/// 002_ARCHITECTURE

Cliffside Architecture · Hanging Temple Wonder

The Wahuang Pavilion is the core building of Wahuang Palace, standing 23 meters tall with four stories, built clinging to the precipitous cliff face. The pavilion is secured to the cliff wall by nine iron chains — when the pavilion is crowded with visitors, the chains tighten under the load, earning it the name "Hanging Temple," a marvel of ancient Chinese architecture.

The Wahuang Pavilion employs traditional Chinese timber frame construction, with flying eaves, carved beams, and painted rafters, showcasing the architectural artistry from the Northern Dynasties through the Ming and Qing periods. Ascending the pavilion and gazing into the distance, one can take in the layered peaks of the Taihang Mountains, while Butian Lake below mirrors the blue sky and white clouds like a bright mirror, refreshing the mind.

This unique construction method of "building in mid-air, secured by chains" not only reflects the extraordinary wisdom and superb skills of ancient craftsmen but also echoes the mythical legend of Nüwa mending the heavens — allowing later generations to contemplate the ancestor's great feat of sky-mending while suspended in the air.

/// 003_SCRIPTURE

Cliff Inscriptions · Cultural Treasure

The Northern Qi cliff inscriptions at Wahuang Palace are the largest surviving Northern Qi cliff-carved scriptures in China, carved between the Tianbao and Wuping eras (550-577 AD), dating back over 1,400 years. The inscribed area covers 165 square meters, containing 6 Buddhist scriptures with over 130,000 characters, renowned as the "Finest Wall Scriptures Under Heaven."

These cliff inscriptions employ the typical Wei Stele calligraphy style of the Northern Dynasties, with powerful strokes and vivid rhythm, representing a precious heritage of Chinese calligraphy and Buddhist culture. The content includes important Buddhist texts such as the Lotus Sutra and the Nirvana Sutra, reflecting the prosperity and popularity of Buddhist culture during the Northern Qi period.

After enduring over a thousand years of wind and rain erosion, these inscriptions remain clearly legible. The exquisite craftsmanship and durable stone quality are truly awe-inspiring, making them a national treasure-level cultural heritage that integrates history, religion, calligraphy, and stone carving art.
