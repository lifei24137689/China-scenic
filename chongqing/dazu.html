Chongqing · Dazu District

◈ World Cultural Heritage · Peak of Grotto ArtDazu Rock Carvings are a collective name for all rock carvings within Dazu District, Chongqing. Represented by the "Five Mountains" — Baodingshan, Beishan, Nanshan, Shimenshan, and Shizhuanshan — it is a World Cultural Heritage site, one of the eight great grottoes of the world, and is hailed as "the jewel in the crown of Chinese grotto art."

Baodingshan Thousand-Hand Guanyin

Baodingshan is the core of Dazu Rock Carvings, carved under the direction of the eminent Southern Song monk Zhao Zhifeng. It features the world-renowned Thousand-Hand Guanyin statue, with 830 hands each in a unique posture and an eye hidden in every palm, displaying unparalleled craftsmanship.

The Sakyamuni Nirvana mural in Great Buddha Bay stretches 31 meters, the largest half-length reclining Buddha in Chinese grotto art. Classic sculptures such as the Perfect Enlightenment Cave, the Three Sages of Huayan, and the Six Realms of Reincarnation perfectly integrate Buddhist teachings with secular life, representing the highest achievement of late Chinese grotto art.

Beishan Carvings Guanyin Statues

Beishan Carvings were first carved in the Tang Dynasty and are renowned for their exquisite craftsmanship and numerous inscriptions. The Guanyin statues here are exceptionally beautiful, earning it the title "Gallery of Chinese Guanyin Statues," presenting a complete evolutionary history of Chinese grotto art from Tang to Song.

With 290 niches and over 4,600 statues, it blends Confucian, Buddhist, and Taoist cultures, showcasing the highest level of grotto art during the Tang and Song periods. The "Revolving Sutra Library" and "Prayer-Bead Guanyin" in Beishan Buddha Bay feature flowing lines and vivid expressions, earning them the reputation of Oriental Beauty Goddesses.

Five Mountains United Three Religions Fusion

Beyond Baodingshan and Beishan, Dazu Rock Carvings also include Nanshan, Shimenshan, and Shizhuanshan — five mountains united to form a World Cultural Heritage site. Nanshan represents Taoist carvings, Shimenshan blends Buddhism and Taoism, and Shizhuanshan merges all three religions, each with unique artistic value.

The carvings across five mountains span a millennium from the Early Tang to the Ming and Qing dynasties, comprising 75 nationally protected cultural heritage sites and over 50,000 statues. Confucianism, Buddhism, and Taoism coexist harmoniously on this land, composing a magnificent finale for Chinese grotto art.
