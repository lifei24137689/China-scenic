Birth of a Holy City

The Jokhang Temple, known in Tibetan as "Tsuglagkhang" or "Jokhang," was first built in 647 AD (21st year of Tang Zhenguan) by King Songtsen Gampo of the Tubo Kingdom to welcome his brides Princess Wencheng and Princess Bhrikuti. Princess Wencheng personally chose the site and summoned craftsmen from Chang'an to participate in its construction — a history spanning 1,378 years.

As the Tibetan saying goes, "Before Jokhang, there was no Lhasa" — revealing the temple's central place in Lhasa's history. The Barkhor Street that formed around Jokhang has for millennia been the sacred circuit for Tibetan pilgrims and the very soul of Lhasa's old town.

HEART OF TIBETAN BUDDHISM

Sanctuary of Faith

Jokhang Temple houses the Jowo Shakyamuni statue — a life-sized image of the Buddha at age 12, brought to Tibet by Princess Wencheng. It is the most sacred Buddha statue in the hearts of Tibetan Buddhist followers. The blue stone floor in front of the temple is deeply worn by the prostrations of countless devotees, while ten thousand butter lamps burn without cease, carrying on a millennial pilgrimage tradition.

Tibetan, Han Chinese, Nepalese, and Indian architectural styles merge here in perfect harmony. The main assembly hall rises four stories with a resplendent golden roof. The outer walls employ the distinctive Tibetan "Bema" grass wall technique, beams and pillars are richly painted, and the corridor murals cover over 2,600 square meters.

Heart of the Old City

Jokhang Temple covers approximately 25,100 square meters in the center of Lhasa's old town. In 1961, it was declared by the State Council as one of the first national-level key cultural heritage protection sites. In 2000, it was inscribed on the UNESCO World Heritage List as an extension of the Potala Palace. In 2025, it was ranked among the top 100 5A scenic area brands.

Three circumambulation routes — Nangkhor, Barkhor, and Lingkhor — encircle Jokhang, creating Lhasa's unique religious and cultural landscape. Every morning and evening, devotees with prayer wheels stream along these paths, their chanting and the spinning of prayer wheels weaving the most moving melody of the ancient city.
