Six Centuries of Sacred Chanting

Tar Temple, known in Tibetan as "Kumbum Jampa Ling," meaning "the Maitreya Temple of Ten Thousand Lion-Roaring Buddha Images," is nestled in the Lianhua Mountain valley of Huangzhong District, Xining City, Qinghai Province. First built in the 12th year of Hongwu reign of the Ming Dynasty (1379 AD), it was established in honor of Tsongkhapa, the founder of the Gelug school (Yellow Hat sect) of Tibetan Buddhism, with a history spanning over six hundred years.

Tar Temple is one of the six major monasteries of the Gelug school of Tibetan Buddhism and the first national 5A-level tourist attraction in Qinghai Province. Built along the mountainside, its halls are arranged in staggered layers, with golden tiles and crimson walls complementing the lush green forests. Known as the "World's Tibetan Academy," it attracts countless devotees and tourists for pilgrimage and sightseeing every year.

Three Artistic Wonders

Tar Temple is world-renowned for its "Three Artistic Wonders" — butter sculpture, mural painting, and applique thangka. Butter sculptures are crafted from yak butter mixed with mineral pigments, shaped into lifelike Buddha figures, flowers, and human forms with exquisite craftsmanship. The annual butter sculpture exhibition on the 15th day of the first lunar month is one of the grandest artistic feasts on the snow-covered plateau.

The temple's murals span over a thousand square meters, painted on hall walls and beams with natural mineral pigments, their vibrant colors remaining unfaded for centuries. Applique thangka uses colored silk and satin cut and stitched together, creating a three-dimensional relief effect, hailed as "Tibetan embroidery thangka." These three masterpieces have all been inscribed on the National Intangible Cultural Heritage list.

Golden Tiles Against the Sky

The Great Gold Tile Hall is the core building of Tar Temple, known in Tibetan as "Sertön," meaning "Golden Tile Hall." Its roof is covered with gilded copper tiles that gleam brilliantly under the plateau sun, radiating grandeur. Inside, the hall enshrines the 11-meter-tall stupa of Tsongkhapa, inlaid with gold, silver, and jewels, including diamonds, agates, and jade — hailed as "the world's most magnificent shrine." In front of the Great Gold Tile Hall stand the Bodhi tree and the Bodhi stupa. Legend has it that Tsongkhapa was born here; his mother buried his umbilical cord in the earth, from which a white sandalwood tree grew, with lion-roaring Buddha images naturally appearing on its leaves — hence the Tibetan name "Kumbum," meaning "ten thousand lion roars."
