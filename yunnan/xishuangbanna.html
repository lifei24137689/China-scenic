An Emerald on the Tropic of Cancer

Founded in 1959 by renowned botanist Professor Cai Xitao, the Xishuangbanna Tropical Botanical Garden of the Chinese Academy of Sciences is located in Menglun Town, Mengla County, Xishuangbanna Dai Autonomous Prefecture, Yunnan Province. It is China's largest tropical botanical garden and a world-class center for tropical plant research.

Spanning approximately 1,125 hectares, the garden sits on Hulu Island, encircled by the Luosuo River, a tributary of the Lancang River (Mekong River). It collects and preserves over 13,000 species of tropical plants, including rare species such as Antiaris toxicaria, Antiaris, and Parashorea chinensis, earning it the reputation of "Noah's Ark of Tropical Plants."

A Vibrant World of Exotic Flora

The garden features 38 specialized collection areas, including Palm Garden, Banyan Garden, Bamboo Garden, Hundred-Flower Garden, and Exotic Plants Garden. From Africa's baobab trees to South America's giant water lilies, from the flavor-changing miracle fruit to the dancing Telegraph Plant — every step is a feast for the eyes and nose.

The Hundred-Flower Garden blooms year-round, while the Exotic Plants Garden never fails to amaze: mimosa leaves fold at a touch, cannonball flowers bloom large and vibrant, and epiphytic orchids burst into magnificent blossoms on tree trunks. This is not only a plant kingdom but also a living classroom of nature education.

The Life Code of the Tropical Rainforest

The Valley Rainforest area is the most pristine part of the garden, preserving a complete tropical rainforest ecosystem. Buttress roots, cauliflory, strangulation, and canopy gardens — all the classic features of tropical rainforests are perfectly displayed here.

This area is also home to Asian elephants, green peacocks, hornbills, and other rare animals. Walking along the canopy walkway, you can overlook the entire rainforest from the treetop level and feel the heartbeat of Earth's oldest ecosystem. The garden also serves as China's center for tropical plant research, making significant contributions to global plant diversity conservation.
