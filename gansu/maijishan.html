Millennia of Grottoes

Maijishan Grottoes were first constructed during the Later Qin dynasty (384–417 AD) and underwent continuous excavation and restoration through more than ten dynasties including Northern Wei, Western Wei, Northern Zhou, Sui, Tang, Five Dynasties, Song, Yuan, Ming, and Qing, spanning over 1,600 years of history. Named for its resemblance to a farmer's grain stack, the solitary peak rises between 1,400 and 1,800 meters above sea level at the western end of the Qinling Mountains.

With 221 existing niches, 10,632 clay and stone sculptures, and 979.54 square meters of murals, it ranks among China's Four Great Grottoes. Its statues are world-renowned for exquisite clay sculpture artistry, earning it the title "Oriental Gallery of Sculptures." In 2014, it was inscribed as part of the "Silk Roads: the Routes Network of Chang'an-Tianshan Corridor" on the World Heritage List.

Masterful Sculpture

Maijishan Grottoes are unrivaled for their exquisite clay sculpture art, with a realistic and expressive style that has earned them the reputation of the "Oriental Gallery of Sculptures." Unlike the murals of Mogao Caves in Dunhuang, the bold stone carvings of Yungang Grottoes, or the delicate reliefs of Longmen Grottoes, Maijishan's clay sculptures focus more on capturing the spirit of the figures, with flowing garment drapery, compassionate countenances, and a profoundly human touch.

The Northern Zhou "thin relief" technique is particularly unique, perfectly combining relief sculpture with painting — an artistic marvel found only at Maijishan. The seated Buddha of the Northern Wei in Cave 44 is hailed as the "Oriental Mona Lisa," its mysterious smile lingering for over a millennium, captivating countless viewers.

Silk Road Radiance

Maijishan stands at a crucial node along the southern route of the ancient Silk Road, serving as an important waystation for Buddhism's eastward transmission. Monks and artisans from the Western Regions blended the essence of Gandhara art with Central Plains aesthetics, creating the distinctive Maijishan sculptural style. The smiling Buddha, the gentle Bodhisattva, and the mighty Heavenly King — each bears witness to the fusion of civilizations.

The scenic area covers 215 square kilometers and was designated a National 5A Tourist Attraction in 2011. Ascending the precipitous cliffside plank roads, overlooking the verdant mountains, and gazing up at thousand-year-old Buddha images — time stands still here. Maijishan is not only a sanctuary of Buddhist art but also an eternal monument to the dialogue of human civilizations.
