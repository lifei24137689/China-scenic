Top Pass Under Heaven

First of the Nine Passes · 3,000 Years of History · First of the Three Outer Passes · Key Section of the Great Wall

Yanmen Pass, located in the Yanmen Mountains, about 20 km north of Dai County, Xinzhou City, Shanxi Province, is one of the oldest and most significant passes along the Great Wall of China, honored as the "Top Pass Under Heaven". As the ancient saying goes, "Of the nine strategic passes under heaven, Yanmen stands first," a testament to its supreme historical status.

With a rich history spanning over 3,000 years, Yanmen Pass has served since antiquity as the first line of defense for the Central Plains dynasties against nomadic incursions from the north. Along with Ningwu Pass and Pian Pass, it forms the "Three Outer Passes" — with Yanmen taking the foremost position, a strategic role beyond compare.

Yanmen Pass is a vital component of the Great Wall. In 2001, it was designated a fifth-batch National Key Cultural Relic Protection Site, and in 2017 it was recognized as a National 5A Tourist Attraction. The scenic area covers 30 square kilometers, and in 2014 it was named "China's Best Cultural Tourism Destination."

Iron Blood Border Pass

Li Mu Guarding the Frontier · Meng Tian's Northern Campaign · Wei Qing & Huo Qubing's Expeditions · Yang Ye Defending Against Liao · Countless Generals Fought Here

Yanmen Pass is one of the most densely battle-scarred passes in Chinese military history. During the Warring States period, the famed Zhao general Li Mu garrisoned here, annihilating over 100,000 Xiongnu cavalry. "Li Mu Guards Yanmen" became a legend that echoes through the ages, and his temple still stands within the pass to this day.

In the Qin dynasty, Meng Tian led 300,000 soldiers north to repel the Xiongnu, building the Great Wall and fortifying the passes — Yanmen was a key defensive line. During the Western Han, Wei Qing and Huo Qubing launched multiple northern expeditions through Yanmen Pass, their warhorses trampling the steppes all the way to the Langjun Xu Mountains. In the Northern Song, Yang Ye (Old Commandant Yang) defended Yanmen, defeating the Liao army against overwhelming odds — the saga of the Yang Family Generals lives on, a tale of heroism and sacrifice.

In the seventh year of Hongwu's reign (1374 CE), the Ming court rebuilt the East Jing Pass Fortress in the Yanmen Mountains. Most of the pass structures that survive today date from the Ming dynasty. Over thousands of years, Yanmen Pass witnessed countless bloody battles — the Han against the Xiongnu, the Tang against the Turks, the Song against the Khitan, the Ming against the Mongols. Every inch of this land is steeped in the blood of warriors.

Millennium Trade Route

Crossroads of Central Plains & Nomadic Cultures · Shanxi Merchants' Route Westward · Witness to Frontier Trade's Rise and Fall

Yanmen Pass was not only a military stronghold but also a vital crossroads where Central Plains and nomadic cultures converged and blended. During times of peace, merchants and travelers passed through the pass in an endless stream, the ringing of camel bells never ceasing. It was a key route for the Shanxi merchants heading westward, where silk, tea, furs, and horses changed hands — a living testament to two thousand years of frontier trade.

Major attractions within the scenic area include the majestic Pass Fortress and Barbican, the Tianxian Gate engraved with the characters "Heavenly Defile," the towering Yan Tower, the Li Mu Temple honoring the Zhao general, the Houling Great Wall snaking along the mountain ridge, and the Ancient Pass Road that has borne a thousand years of memories — each site tells a story of frontier fortress culture, Great Wall culture, and pass culture.

Walking along Yanmen Pass's ancient roadway, the flagstones beneath your feet have been polished to a mirror-like sheen by a millennium of camel and horse traffic. The arrow marks and sword scars on the fortress walls seem to whisper tales of armored cavalry from ages past. This "Top Pass Under Heaven" embodies the perfect fusion of military, trade, and cultural identities.
