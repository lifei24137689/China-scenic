Sacred Buddhist Realm

Mount Wutai ranks first among the Four Sacred Buddhist Mountains of China and is recognized alongside Lumbini (Nepal), Sarnath, Bodh Gaya, and Kushinagar (India) as one of the Five Sacred Buddhist Sites of the world. It is the only sacred site where Chinese Buddhism (Green Temples) and Tibetan Buddhism (Yellow Temples) coexist in harmony. As the manifested abode of Manjusri Bodhisattva, its incense has flourished for millennia.

World Heritage Inscribed as a Cultural Landscape on the World Heritage List in 2009
5A Rating Designated a National 5A Tourist Attraction in 2007
Buddhist Status Foremost of the Four Sacred Buddhist Mountains · One of the Five Sacred Buddhist Sites

/// 02

Roof of North China

Mount Wutai comprises five terraced peaks: East Peak Wanghai, West Peak Guayue, South Peak Jinxiu, North Peak Yedou, and Central Peak Cuiyan. The five peaks rise steeply with treeless summits resembling terraced platforms, hence the name "Wutai" (Five Terraces). North Peak Yedou, at 3,061 meters above sea level, stands as the highest point in northern China, earning the illustrious title "Roof of North China."

Total Area 2,837 square kilometers
Scenic Area 607 square kilometers
Highest Peak North Peak Yedou at 3,061 m
Architectural Treasures
/// 03

Architectural Treasures

Mount Wutai preserves 86 existing temples (within and beyond the five terraces), including 13 Major Historical and Cultural Sites Protected at the National Level. Xiantong Temple is the founding monastery, the White Pagoda of Tayuan Temple serves as the iconic landmark of Mount Wutai, and Pusading Temple is a Qing dynasty imperial monastery. Since the first temple was built during the Yongping era of the Eastern Han dynasty, spanning nearly two millennia, the site presents a complete architectural sequence from the Northern Wei to the Qing dynasties.

Existing Temples 86 (within and beyond the five terraces)
National Sites 13 Major Historical and Cultural Sites Protected at the National Level
