/// 01

Imperial Caves

Yungang Grottoes is the only large-scale cave complex in China built under imperial patronage. Construction began during the Heping era of the Northern Wei dynasty (5th century AD), lasted 64 years, and stretches about 1 km from east to west. As a royal project of the Northern Wei, its grand scale and exquisite carvings were unparalleled in their time.

The Tanyao Five Caves (Caves 16–20) represent the early period, with five giant Buddha statues symbolizing five Northern Wei emperors, merging imperial authority with divine power. Carved into the sandstone of Wuzhou Mountain, master craftsmen transformed solid rock into a Buddhist kingdom, pioneering cave art in the Central Plains.

/// 02

Ten Thousand Buddhas

Yungang Grottoes currently has 45 major caves, 209 subsidiary caves, 252 niches of various sizes, and over 59,000 Buddha statues. The total carved area exceeds 18,000 square meters, with the tallest figure reaching 17 meters and the smallest merely 2 centimeters — a breathtaking spectacle.

The middle-period caves are renowned for their exquisite detailing. The walls are densely carved with thousands of Buddhas, apsaras, musicians, lotus and honeysuckle patterns layered in rich complexity, showcasing the pinnacle of stone carving craftsmanship and Buddhist art during the Northern Wei's golden age.

/// 03

Silk Road Gem

Yungang Grottoes bears witness to the historical transmission of Indian and Central Asian Buddhist art into China, where diverse sculptural styles converged. Early works show influences from Gandhara and Mathura art; the middle period blended these with traditional Chinese aesthetics; and the later period developed a native style known as "slender bones and pure features."

As the crown of 5th-century Chinese stone carving art, it was designated one of the first Major Historical and Cultural Sites Protected at the National Level in 1961, inscribed on the UNESCO World Heritage List on December 14, 2001, and rated a National 5A Scenic Spot in 2007.
