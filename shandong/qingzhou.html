Ou Garden

Finest Garden North of the Yangtze

Ou Garden, originally the private garden of Feng Pu, Grand Secretary of the Wenhua Hall during the Kangxi reign of the Qing Dynasty, is renowned as a "famous garden north of the Yangtze." Its rockeries were crafted by the master rockery artist Zhang Ran, exquisite and ingenious, standing as a northern exemplar of Chinese garden art.

Within the garden, lush trees, scattered pavilions, and rockeries with ponds create delightful scenes. The four-character stone screens of "Fu" (Blessing), "Shou" (Longevity), "Kang" (Health), and "Ning" (Tranquility) are the garden's treasures, embodying the profound heritage of Chinese traditional culture and the spiritual aspirations of literati.

Qingzhou Museum

Seven Millennia of Civilization

The Qingzhou Museum is China's only county-level national first-class museum, housing over 40,000 cultural relics. The most stunning are the Buddhist statues unearthed from the Longxing Temple site, hailed as "a major discovery that rewrites the history of Eastern art."

Its collection spans from the Neolithic Age to the Ming and Qing dynasties, fully presenting the 7,000-year thread of Qingzhou civilization. The stone Buddhist statues from the Northern Wei to Northern Song dynasties bear gentle smiles, known as the "Qingzhou Smile," with artistic value unrivaled in the world.

Zhaode Ancient Street

Millennia-Old Street Endures

Zhaode Ancient Street is one of the best-preserved Ming-Qing streets in Qingzhou Ancient City, with blue flagstone roads winding for hundreds of meters flanked by rows of historic buildings. Once a bustling hub of merchants, it bears witness to Qingzhou's glorious history as a major commercial center in the East.

Strolling along the street, every step treads upon seven hundred years of history. The Zhenjiao Mosque stands along the way, blending traditional Chinese architecture with Islamic cultural elements — a nationally protected cultural heritage site and a precious testament to multicultural integration.
