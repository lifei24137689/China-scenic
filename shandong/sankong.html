Confucius Temple

The Confucius Temple is the temple where successive feudal dynasties worshipped Confucius. It is located in the center of Qufu. Construction began in 478 BC, the year after Confucius' death, using three rooms of his former residence as the temple. Emperors of later dynasties continuously expanded it, forming a grand architectural complex with nine courtyards arranged along a north-south central axis.

Dacheng Hall, the main hall of the Confucius Temple, is known as one of the "Three Great Eastern Halls," alongside the Hall of Supreme Harmony in Beijing's Forbidden City and the Tiankuang Hall at Dai Temple in Tai'an. The ten front eave pillars feature deep-relief coiling dragon carvings, with dragons soaring in majestic postures — a pair of dragons facing each other on each pillar, forming a rare stone carving artistic treasure in the world.

Confucius Mansion

The Confucius Mansion, also known as the "Mansion of the Duke Yansheng," is located east of the Confucius Temple. It was the hereditary residence of the eldest direct descendants of Confucius. Since the Song Dynasty, Confucius' descendants were granted the title "Duke Yansheng," a hereditary rank passed down for nearly a thousand years, making it the only aristocratic mansion in Chinese feudal society that continued uninterrupted through dynastic changes.

The Confucius Mansion covers approximately 160,000 square meters, with 463 rooms including halls, chambers, towers, and pavilions. It is laid out in three routes: central, eastern, and western. The front served as the official mansion, while the rear was the private residence. It is one of the largest and most luxurious surviving feudal aristocratic mansions in China, hailed as "the No. 1 Family Under Heaven."

Confucius Forest

The Confucius Forest, also known as the "Supreme Sage Forest," is the dedicated cemetery for Confucius and his family, located north of Qufu, covering nearly 2,000,000 square meters. After Confucius' death, his disciples brought rare trees from all directions to plant here. Over more than 2,400 years, it has become the longest-lasting and largest family cemetery in the world.

The forest contains over 100,000 trees, with ancient cypresses reaching to the sky, numerous steles and tablets, and stone ceremonial figures standing in groups. Confucius' tomb is located in the center of the forest, with a Ming Dynasty stele inscribed "Tomb of the Great Sage and Civil King" placed in front. Guo Moruo once praised it: "This is a fine natural museum and also a chronicle of the Confucius clan."
