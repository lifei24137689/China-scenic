Yellow River Estuary

The Yellow River, the mother river of the Chinese nation, surges 5,464 kilometers before merging into the Bohai Sea here. The magnificent spectacle where yellow meets blue is a rare natural geographic phenomenon — the muddy yellow river water and the azure sea water converge in a stirring collision, forming a clear "sea-river boundary line" that winds its way to the horizon — breathtaking and soul-stirring.

From the Yuanwang Tower observation deck, the spectacular vista of the great river rushing into the sea unfolds before your eyes. Especially during the autumn high-water season, the river surges forth with sediment, forming a vast fan-shaped deposit at the estuary — like a long letter written by the land to the ocean. This is the final chapter of the Yellow River's story, and the beginning of an ever-changing landscape.

Bird International Airport

The Yellow River Estuary Wetland is the most important stopover and feeding ground along the East Asian–Australasian Flyway for migratory birds, earning it the reputation of "Bird International Airport." Every spring and autumn, millions of migratory birds rest, feed, and roost here. A total of 371 bird species have been recorded, including 26 species under national first-class protection, such as red-crowned cranes, oriental storks, and great bustards.

Bird Island is the ultimate birdwatching destination. From the island, you can see egrets dancing in the reed marshes and gulls gathering in flocks. Vast flocks of birds dance against the sunset, creating a magnificent symphony of life. In winter, thousands of swans winter here, making this wetland a true migratory bird paradise and a world-class birdwatching sanctuary.

Natural Willow Forest · World's Youngest Wetland

The Yellow River Estuary Natural Willow Forest is one of China's largest natural tamarisk and dryland willow forests, covering approximately 12,000 hectares. Strolling along the boardwalk through the willow forest, you'll see ancient willows with gnarled branches and deep roots in the wetland, winding waterways, and swaying reeds — together forming a rustic wetland landscape painting.

The Yellow River carries approximately 1 billion tons of sediment into the sea each year, creating about 25–30 square kilometers of new land annually, making this the world's fastest-growing and youngest wetland ecosystem. With a history of only about 150 years, this young wetland showcases nature's boundless vitality and creativity — an invaluable natural laboratory for geological and ecological research.
