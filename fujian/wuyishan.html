/// 01

Crimson Cliffs & Emerald Waters

Located in northern Fujian, Wuyi Mountain is one of the most celebrated mountain ranges in Southeast China. With a geological history spanning 150 million years, it is renowned for its classic Danxia landforms — red cliffs, solitary peaks, and winding streams. This unique landscape is famously described as "crimson cliffs and emerald waters, three threes and six sixes" — the "three threes" referring to the Nine-Bend Stream, and the "six sixes" to the thirty-six peaks.

In 1999, Wuyi Mountain was inscribed as a UNESCO World Cultural and Natural Heritage site, becoming China's 4th dual heritage property. Its highest peak, Huanggang Mountain, rises 2,158 meters — the tallest summit in East China, earning it the title "Roof of East China."

Nine-Bend Stream Winding Through Peaks

The Nine-Bend Stream is the soul of Wuyi Mountain. Stretching 62.8 kilometers, the stream meanders through the scenic area in nine great bends from west to east, hence its name. The crystal-clear water mirrors the peaks on both banks, and a bamboo raft ride downstream lets you experience the famous verse: "bend after bend, mountains turn; peak after peak, waters embrace."

Bamboo rafting is a must-do activity for visitors. Along the way, you can admire core landmarks such as Jade Maiden Peak, Great King Peak, and Tianyou Peak. It's like drifting through a living landscape painting — as the local saying goes, "If you haven't taken a bamboo raft, you haven't truly seen Wuyi."

Wuyi Rock Tea Rock Essence & Floral Aroma

Wuyi Mountain is the birthplace of Wuyi Rock Tea. Tea bushes growing in the crevices of Danxia cliffs absorb mineral essence from the rock, producing a distinctive "rock essence and floral aroma" flavor. Among them, Da Hong Pao is hailed as the "King of Teas." Only six mother trees remain, growing on the Jiulongke Danxia cliffs, over 360 years old, now under national key protection.

Beyond Da Hong Pao, other rock tea varieties such as Rou Gui and Shui Xian have also earned lasting renown. Tea culture has thrived here for millennia — picking, processing, and tasting tea are integral to Wuyi Mountain's cultural landscape, drawing countless tea enthusiasts from around the world.
