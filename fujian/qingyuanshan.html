Laojun Rock The Greatest Laozi

The Laojun Rock statue is a Song Dynasty carved Daoist seated image of Laozi, measuring 5.63 m in height, 8.01 m in width, and 6.85 m in depth. It is the largest surviving Daoist stone carving in China. Carved from natural rock, the statue features flowing robes, a serene expression, and remarkable lifelikeness, earning it the reputation of "Laozi, the Greatest Under Heaven."

Laojun Rock is not only the iconic landmark of Qingyuan Mountain but also an important heritage of Chinese Daoist culture. The meticulous carving and smooth lines showcase the superb stone sculpture artistry of the Song Dynasty.

Kaiyuan Temple Twin Pagodas

Kaiyuan Temple, the largest Buddhist monastery in Fujian Province, was first built in 686 AD during the Tang Dynasty. Its iconic East and West Pagodas are the tallest surviving stone pagodas in China. The East Pagoda (Zhenguo Pagoda) stands 48.24 m tall, and the West Pagoda (Renshou Pagoda) stands 45.06 m tall — both are masterpieces of Song Dynasty architecture.

Having weathered over a thousand years of storms and earthquakes, the twin pagodas stand as enduring witnesses to Quanzhou's Maritime Silk Road heritage. Each level is adorned with exquisite Buddhist carvings and reliefs, showcasing the remarkable architectural achievements of the Song Dynasty.

Cultural Sanctuary Religious Fusion

Qingyuan Mountain is a sanctuary where Confucianism, Daoism, and Buddhism converge. The mountain is home to the Daoist Laojun Rock, the Buddhist Mituo Rock and Ruixiang Rock, and the Qingjing Mosque at its foot — embodying Quanzhou's distinctive tradition of multicultural coexistence.

The mountain also features numerous cliff inscriptions recording poems and writings by literati across dynasties, serving as invaluable sources for studying Quanzhou's history and culture. Qingyuan Mountain is not only a celebrated natural landmark but also a renowned cultural mountain.
