/// 01

Tulou Wonder Oriental Castle

Fujian Tulou is mainly distributed in Yongding, Nanjing and other areas, representing a unique large-scale rammed earth residential architecture in mountainous regions worldwide. Using raw earth as the primary building material, supplemented by wood and stone, these multi-story circular or square structures are considered marvels in architectural history.

Tulou served not only as dwellings but also as fortresses where Hakka clans lived together and defended against external threats. The ingenious interior layout and complete facilities reflect the Hakka people's wisdom of unity and mutual assistance. In 2008, Fujian Tulou was inscribed on the UNESCO World Heritage List.

/// 02

Architectural Art Rammed Earth Wisdom

The architectural craftsmanship of Fujian Tulou is exceptional. Built with raw earth, sand, gravel, bamboo, and wood using ramming techniques, the walls are sturdy, durable, and highly earthquake-resistant. Circular tulou are particularly unique, appearing from above like giant mushrooms or UFOs scattered across the mountains.

Chengqi Tulou, the representative of Yongding tulou, is hailed as the "King of Tulou" with a diameter of 73 meters, 4 stories, over 400 rooms, and once housing more than 800 people. Zhencheng Tulou, known as the "Prince of Tulou," is renowned for its symmetrical layout and exquisite interior decoration.

Hakka Culture Migration & Heritage

Tulou is an important carrier of Hakka culture. The Hakka are an ethnic group formed by the southward migration of ancient Han Chinese from the Central Plains. During their migration, they built tulou as defensive fortifications and dwellings. Tulou not only embodies the Hakka's architectural wisdom but also carries their historical memory and cultural heritage.

Within the tulou, strong clan values, clear generational hierarchy, and many traditional customs are preserved. The ancestral halls, schools, wells, and other facilities within form a self-sufficient micro-society. Today, tulou is not only a historical site but also a symbol of the Hakka people's spiritual home.
