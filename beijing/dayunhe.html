Gateway to the Capital
Millennium Canal Hub

Stretching nearly 1,800 km, the Beijing-Hangzhou Grand Canal is the longest man-made waterway in the world. Inscribed as a UNESCO World Heritage site in 2014, the Tongzhou section of Beijing marks the northern terminus. Since the Yuan Dynasty, it served as the empire's grain-transport artery — immortalized in the classic couplet: "South Tongzhou, North Tongzhou, south and north Tongzhou connect south and north."

This scenic area follows the canal's course, linking Canal Park, the Beacon Tower, the Grain Transport Dock, the Grand Canal Museum, and the Green Heart Park. It blends a millennium of canal culture with modern ecological recreation, making it one of Beijing's most culturally rich and naturally beautiful destinations.

GRAND CANAL MUSEUM

Under the Sails
A Temple of Canal Civilization

Designed as a "Canal Boat," the Beijing Grand Canal Museum (East Wing of the Capital Museum) resembles a giant vessel docked along the waterway. Inside, it houses over 10,000 artifacts excavated along the canal, systematically presenting more than 2,500 years of its excavation and transformation.

To its east lies the Green Heart Park, while to the west stretches the canal's blue waves. Thousands of visitors come each day to trace the millennia-old water-transport heritage and read this "living epic" flowing through the land of China.

GREEN HEART PARK

Green Lung
The Canal Reborn

Covering approximately 7.13 km², the Grand Canal Forest Park stretches for miles along both banks. Its layout — "one river, two banks, six gardens, eighteen scenes" — seamlessly blends historic canal relics, wetland ecology, and modern landscape design.

In spring, peach blossoms and willows burst into color; in autumn, reeds turn golden. The Beacon Tower stands tall, witnessing Tongzhou's centuries of canal prosperity and today's ecological renaissance. Cycling, camping, birdwatching, flower appreciation — this park has become the most enchanting green corridor in Beijing's sub-center.
