OVERVIEW

A World Wonder
The Spine of China

The Great Wall is ancient China's greatest defensive project, first built during the Spring and Autumn period and continuously expanded over two millennia. The Badaling section is the best-preserved and most representative stretch of the Ming Great Wall, strategically situated with formidable passes, earning its reputation as "the essence of the Great Wall".

The Mutianyu section is renowned for its lush vegetation and dense watchtowers. Visitors from around the world can marvel at the Wall's majestic grandeur while immersing themselves in nature's tranquility. In 1987, the Great Wall was inscribed as a UNESCO World Heritage Site.

BADALING

Key to the Northern Gate
The Peril of Juyong

Badaling Great Wall sits at an elevation of 1,015 meters. Since ancient times, it has been said that "the danger of Juyong Pass lies not in the pass itself, but in Badaling." The fortress wall, trapezoidal in shape, follows the contours of the mountain, standing tall and robust with an average height of 7.8 meters.

The Badaling section is densely dotted with watchtowers. North Tower No. 8 is the highest point — from its summit, the Great Wall winds like a mighty dragon across the rugged mountain ranges. This section has hosted more foreign heads of state than any other, serving as China's national guest scenic area.

MUTIANYU

Double-Sided Battlements
A Fortress in the Forest

Located in Huairou District, the Mutianyu Great Wall boasts a forest coverage rate of over 96%, offering strikingly different scenery across seasons — mountain flowers bloom in spring, while red leaves blanket the hills in autumn. Its most distinctive feature is the double-sided battlements, an extremely rare design along the Great Wall.

The Mutianyu section features densely packed watchtowers and uniquely designed gate platforms. The main gate platform, where three watchtowers stand side by side, is considered a masterpiece of Great Wall architecture. It is also the most popular section among international visitors, where Eastern and Western civilizations converge.
