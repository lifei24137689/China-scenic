OVERVIEW

The Great Ming Dynasty
The Eternal Resting Place

The Ming Tombs are located at the foot of Tianshou Mountain in Changping, Beijing. They form the burial complex of thirteen Ming emperors after the capital was moved to Beijing. Construction began with Changling in 1409 (Yongle 7th year) and ended with Siling under Emperor Chongzhen, spanning over 230 years. It is the best-preserved imperial tomb complex in China and the world, burying the largest number of emperors.

The tomb area covers approximately 120 square kilometers, laid out in a rigorous layout following the terrain. Each tomb is independent yet connected by the Sacred Way, forming a magnificent imperial burial system built according to the ritual of "court in front, tomb behind."

SACRED WAY

Stone Statue Procession
A Thousand-Year Sigh

The Sacred Way is the soul axis of the Ming Tombs, stretching approximately 7.3 kilometers from south to north through the tomb area. Lining both sides of the Sacred Way stand 24 stone beasts (lions, xiezhi, camels, elephants, qilin, horses) and 12 stone figures (warriors, civil officials, meritorious officials), collectively known as the "Stone Statues," symbolizing the emperor's ceremonial guard in life.

Each stone statue is carved from a single block of bluish-white stone, with the heaviest weighing over tens of tons. The sculptures are primitive and vigorous in style, solemn and dignified in expression. Over six hundred years of wind and rain erosion, the chisel marks and moss stains intertwine on the stones, like frozen imprints of time from the Great Ming Dynasty.

CHANGLING

Emperor Yongle
Foundation for Eternity

Changling is the joint burial tomb of Emperor Yongle (Zhu Di) and Empress Renxiao Wen (Xu), situated beneath the main peak of Tianshou Mountain. It is the largest in scale, earliest built, and best preserved among the thirteen tombs. Its Ling'en Hall spans nine bays wide and five bays deep, rivaling the Hall of Supreme Harmony in the Forbidden City. The 60 giant nanmu pillars inside the hall are truly breathtaking.

The remaining twelve tombs were built surrounding Changling, forming a pattern of stars surrounding the moon, highlighting Emperor Yongle's central position in Ming Dynasty history. Changling is not only the "first tomb" of the Ming Tombs but also the pinnacle of ancient Chinese imperial mausoleum architecture.

DINGLING

Underground Palace
Wanli's Hidden Treasure

Dingling is the tomb of Emperor Wanli (Zhu Yijun), the only imperial tomb among the thirteen that has been archaeologically excavated. The underground palace lies 27 meters beneath the mound, consisting of five stone halls (front, central, rear, left, and right) with a total area of 1,195 square meters. Entirely vaulted with stone without beams or pillars, it is a marvel of Ming Dynasty underground architecture.

Over 3,000 artifacts have been unearthed from the underground palace, including the golden wing coronet of Emperor Wanli, phoenix crowns, brocade robes, and numerous gold and silver vessels. These are now displayed at the Dingling Museum, revealing the mysterious luxury of Ming imperial life to the world.
