OVERVIEW

Qing Dynasty Mansion
Peerless Elegance

Prince Gong's Mansion, located by Shichahai Lake in Beijing, is the largest princely mansion complex of the Qing Dynasty. Originally the residence of Heshen, a powerful minister during the Qianlong reign, it later passed through two subsequent owners — Prince Qing Yonglin and Prince Gong Yixin — witnessing the entire trajectory of the Qing Dynasty from prosperity to decline. Hence the saying, "Half of Qing history is told within the walls of Prince Gong's Mansion."

The mansion covers approximately 60,000 square meters, comprising two sections: the main residence and the garden. The main buildings are stately and solemn, while the garden blends the grace of Jiangnan gardens with northern Chinese architecture, with pavilions, terraces, and towers arranged in exquisite harmony — a masterpiece of princely mansion construction in the Qing Dynasty.

SILVER HALL

A Minister's Residence
Fit for Royalty

The Silver Hall is the main hall of Prince Gong's Mansion and the highest-ranking building within the complex. Five bays wide, topped with green glazed tiles and adorned with ridge beasts, it exudes the imperial prestige granted by special decree. A broad moon platform extends before the hall, guarded by stone lions on both sides, where the prince would ascend to receive homage during grand ceremonies.

During the Qianlong reign, the powerful minister Heshen spent over twenty years meticulously building this residence into a lavish palace. When he was eventually impeached and his property confiscated, the total value of his assets amounted to between 800 million and 1.1 billion taels of silver — exceeding fifteen years of the Qing government's fiscal revenue. Hence the proverb: "When Heshen falls, Jiaqing feasts."

BACK TOWER

Treasure Tower
East Meets West

The Rear Tower is the most enigmatic building in Prince Gong's Mansion, stretching over 160 meters in length with a total of 108 rooms across two floors. Legend has it that Heshen used this as a treasure vault, with each room's window fashioned in a unique shape to indicate the type of treasure stored within — round for gold, square for silver, and diamond-shaped for jade.

At the garden entrance behind the tower stands the country's only white marble Western-style gate, built by Prince Gong Yixin in imitation of the Western-style pavilions at the Old Summer Palace. Rococo-style ornamentation blends seamlessly with traditional Chinese brick carvings, making it a precious testament to the fusion of Chinese and Western cultures in the Qing Dynasty.

GARDEN

Cuijin Garden
The World of Fu

The garden of Prince Gong's Mansion — Cuijin Garden — covers 28,000 square meters, blending the gentle grace of Jiangnan gardens with the open grandeur of northern Chinese landscaping. Pavilions, terraces, corridors, rockeries, and waterways are arranged with exquisite precision. The Grand Theater, the Moon-Viewing Terrace, and the Bat Hall each possess their own distinctive charm.

Inside the Miyun Cave of the garden lies a stele bearing the character "Fu" (Blessing) in the calligraphy of Emperor Kangxi, written by the emperor himself to pray for longevity for his grandmother, Empress Dowager Xiaozhuang. The bold and vigorous strokes of the character carry five layers of blessing: "many sons, many talents, many fields, many years, and much happiness." Prince Gong's Mansion is therefore celebrated as the "Garden of Ten Thousand Blessings."
