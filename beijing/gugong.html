OVERVIEW · AT A GLANCE

Supreme Sovereignty
Center of the Realm

Founded in 1925, the Palace Museum is based on the imperial palace of the Ming and Qing dynasties — the Forbidden City. Covering 720,000 square meters with a floor area of approximately 150,000 square meters, it is the largest and best-preserved wooden architectural complex in the world.

Inscribed as a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 1987. Its collection exceeds 1.95 million pieces (sets), spanning 25 categories including paintings, ceramics, bronzes, and timepieces. It is China's largest museum of ancient art and culture, and the most iconic cultural landmark of Chinese civilization.

MERIDIAN GATE

Five Phoenix Towers
Gateway to the Empire

The Meridian Gate is the main entrance to the Forbidden City, first built in the 18th year of Yongle (1420). Its U-shaped plan features five towering pavilions resembling five phoenixes spreading their wings, earning it the name "Five Phoenix Tower." During the Qing dynasty, civil and military officials would queue here for imperial audiences; triumphant armies held victory ceremonies at this very gate.

Passing through the Meridian Gate, five white marble bridges arch across the Golden Water River like rainbows. The Gate of Supreme Harmony stands majestically ahead, beyond which lies the largest existing wooden hall in China — the Hall of Supreme Harmony, the axial heart of the Forbidden City complex.

HALL OF SUPREME HARMONY · TAIHEDIAN

The Golden Throne Hall
Supreme Imperial Power

The Hall of Supreme Harmony — commonly known as the "Golden Throne Hall" — is the highest-ranking ancient architectural structure in China. On the marble terrace before it stand a sundial, a grain measure, bronze tortoises, and bronze cranes. Inside, a golden-coffered ceiling and a gilded cloud-and-dragon throne embody imperial majesty at its peak.

The Palace Museum houses over 1.95 million pieces (sets) of precious artifacts. National treasures such as Along the River During the Qingming Festival, A Thousand Li of Rivers and Mountains, and the various-glazed ceremonial vases silently narrate the splendor of Chinese civilization. The Forbidden City itself is a living, three-dimensional history of Chinese architecture.

IMPERIAL GARDEN

Depths of the Palace
Where Time Stands Still

The Imperial Garden lies behind the Palace of Earthly Tranquility, marking the northern terminus of the Forbidden City's central axis. Here, ancient cypresses scrape the sky, fantastical rockeries pile into layered peaks, and the Pavilion of Ten Thousand Springs stands opposite the Pavilion of a Thousand Autumns. Six-hundred-year-old ginkgo trees and dragon-claw pagoda trees have witnessed the daily lives of Ming and Qing emperors and empresses.

The Treasure Gallery and Clock and Watch Gallery are among the museum's most dazzling collections — phoenix coronets and embroidered capes, the Golden Vessel of Eternal Unity, the copper-gilt writing automaton clock… each piece embodies the supreme craftsmanship of ancient Chinese artisans.
