Prince Palace

Jingjiang Prince Palace

The Jingjiang Prince Palace was built in the fifth year of the Hongwu reign of the Ming Dynasty (1372 AD), 44 years earlier than the Forbidden City in Beijing. It is the largest and best-preserved Ming Dynasty princely palace in China. The palace covers about 20 hectares, with a city wall approximately 1.5 km in circumference, featuring red walls and yellow glazed tiles in a magnificent style.

As the residence of the Jingjiang princes of the Ming Dynasty, it was home to 14 generations of Jingjiang princes over 280 years. The palace features a rigorous architectural layout with richly decorated beams and pillars, fully reflecting the artistic achievements of Ming imperial architecture. Today, Prince City has become an important carrier of Guilin's historical culture.

Solitary Peak

Solitary Beauty Peak is located within the Jingjiang Prince Palace, with an elevation of 216 meters and a relative height of 66 meters, making it the highest point in Guilin's urban area. Rising abruptly as a solitary peak, steep and lofty, it has long been known as the "Pillar Under Heaven." Climbing 306 steps leads to the summit, offering a panoramic view of Guilin's mountain and river scenery.

The peak is composed of limestone, shaped by hundreds of millions of years of wind and rain erosion, forming a unique karst solitary peak landform. There are 136 cliff inscriptions from various dynasties on the mountain, among which the most famous is the original source of the timeless phrase "Guilin's landscapes reign supreme under heaven."

Pillar Under Heaven Cliff Inscriptions City Landmark

Examination Hall

The Guangxi Examination Hall within the Prince City was the venue for the provincial imperial examinations during the Qing Dynasty. From the early Qing period until the abolition of the imperial examination system in the 31st year of the Guangxu reign (1905 AD), it hosted examinations for over 200 years, producing countless successful candidates and scholars, making it an important symbol of Guangxi's cultural heritage.

The examination hall complex is well-preserved, with neatly arranged examination cells, allowing visitors to feel as if they have traveled back to the era of imperial examinations a century ago. Standing within the hall, one can sense the weight and solemnity of ancient China's imperial examination culture.
