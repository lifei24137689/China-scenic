The Hundred-li Lijiang River

The Lijiang River originates from Mount Mao'er in northeastern Guilin, stretching about 164 kilometers. It flows through Guilin, Yangshuo, and other areas, embodying the essence of Guilin's landscapes. Along its banks, majestic peaks rise sharply, emerald waters meander, and green mountains are reflected in the clear ripples, creating a magnificent painting where "boats sail on blue waves, and people wander within a picture."

Centered on karst landforms, the Lijiang River Scenic Area features the world's most typical peaks and peak-cluster landscapes. It was designated a National Key Scenic Area in 1982 and rated a National 5A Tourist Attraction in 2007.

Nine-Horse Mountain

Nine-Horse Mountain is one of the most famous sights along the Lijiang River, located near Xingping Ancient Town. The cliff face looks as if it were carved by knives and axes, standing about 200 meters high and 100 meters wide. Naturally formed on the rock wall are images of nine gallant horses — some standing, some lying, some galloping, some leaping — incredibly lifelike.

A local folk rhyme goes: "Look, young horse-spotter, tell me how many horses there are? Spot seven and you're a top scholar, spot nine and you're the champion." Over the centuries, countless poets and literati have left behind poems and legendary tales inspired by this site.

Yellow Cloth Reflection

Yellow Cloth Reflection is located in the prime section of the Lijiang River. It gets its name from a massive yellowish-brown stone slab on the riverbed that spreads like a yellow cloth. Here the river is wide and the current gentle. The green mountains and lush trees along both banks are reflected in the crystal-clear, mirror-like water, blurring the line between reality and illusion — dreamlike and enchanting.

This spot is the backdrop for the 20-yuan banknote and one of the best photography locations along the Lijiang River. On sunny days, with blue skies, white clouds, green peaks, and emerald bamboo mirrored in the water, it becomes hard to tell where the mountain ends and its reflection begins — a beauty beyond words.
