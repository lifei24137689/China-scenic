Teng Wang Pavilion

Jiangxi Province · Nanchang City

CHAPTER I

Millennium Tower · The Grand Pavilion

Tengwang Pavilion was first built in the fourth year of Yonghui of the Tang Dynasty (653 AD) by Li Yuanying, Prince Teng of Tang, younger brother of Emperor Taizong Li Shimin. Standing 57.5 meters tall, it features a unique "three visible, seven hidden" layout — appearing to have three stories from the outside, but actually seven inside, with soaring eaves, green tiles, and crimson pillars, creating a magnificent presence.

Along with the Yellow Crane Tower and Yueyang Tower, it is known as one of the "Three Great Towers of the South," and ranks first among them for its profound cultural heritage and splendid architectural style. From the top, the Gan River looks like a silk ribbon, the western mountains rise in layered green, and the entire city of Nanchang unfolds beneath you.

"Falling clouds fly together with a lone wild duck, autumn waters merge with the vast sky in one hue. Fishing boats sing at dusk, their sounds echoing to the shores of Pengli; wild geese startle at the cold, their cries fading by the banks of Hengyang."
— Wang Bo, "Preface to the Tengwang Pavilion"
CHAPTER II

Wang Bo and the Preface · A Masterpiece for the Ages

In the second year of Shangyuan of Emperor Gaozong of Tang (675 AD), Wang Bo passed through Nanchang just as the Tengwang Pavilion's renovation was completed. At the banquet, Wang Bo improvised the timeless masterpiece "Preface to the Tengwang Pavilion." The 773-character essay dazzles with ornate diction and profound imagery — "Falling clouds fly together with a lone wild duck, autumn waters merge with the vast sky in one hue" became an eternal verse.

The pavilion houses a treasured collection of poems and inscriptions by literati from various dynasties, with Su Shi's handwritten "Preface to the Tengwang Pavilion" stele being especially precious. Also on display is the large white marble relief "The Favorable Wind That Brought Wang Bo to Tengwang Pavilion," vividly recreating the historical moment when Wang Bo composed his famous preface.

CHAPTER III

Twenty-Nine Reconstructions · Reborn from Ashes

Over more than 1,300 years of history, Tengwang Pavilion was destroyed by war and natural disasters 29 times, and rose from the ashes 29 times. Each reconstruction embodies the Chinese people's dedication to cultural heritage. The current pavilion was completed on October 8, 1989 (the Double Ninth Festival) as the 29th reconstruction, designed by the renowned architect Liang Sicheng.

Today, Tengwang Pavilion is not only a historical and cultural landmark but also the city card of Nanchang. When night falls, the illuminated Tengwang Pavilion shines brilliantly, complementing the modern buildings along the Gan River — a breathtaking fusion of ancient and contemporary.
